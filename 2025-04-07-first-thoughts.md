I have three journals under my bed. A fourth in the drawer of my bedside table, and a fifth — half-finished, its spine cracked — on the shelf between a dictionary and a book about Japanese carpentry I've never actually read.

For years, writing was private. A way to process, not to communicate. The idea of showing it to anyone produced the same mild nausea as the idea of reading my diary aloud at a bus stop.

And then, for reasons I'm still not entirely sure about, I published something.

## The first Monday

It was a short piece about walking home in the rain and thinking about my grandfather. About 600 words. I put it on a page with no design and no promotion and shared the link with three people I trusted.

Something shifted.

Not because people read it — three people did, and they were polite about it — but because the act of writing it had changed. I'd written it knowing, at some level, that another mind might encounter it. That constraint made me more honest, not less. I couldn't be vague or self-indulgent the way I was in my journals, because I had to be understood.

> The best writing isn't the most private or the most public. It's the kind where you're genuinely trying to say something true to someone who isn't you.

## Why a weekly schedule?

Because indefinite timelines produce nothing. I know this about myself. If I say "I'll write when I have something to say," I'll wait for a feeling of readiness that never quite arrives.

A weekly schedule is a forcing function. It says: you have until Monday. Figure out what you think.

Some weeks the deadline produces something I'm proud of. Some weeks it produces something I post anyway, with the low-grade discomfort of knowing it's not quite there. But it always produces *something*, and something is infinitely more than nothing.

If you've found this — welcome. I'm glad you're here.
